One rule (Reading Cadre) has a known, unresolved conflict between the Calculator's current logic and the FY2026 appropriations act (HB 225 §322(4)), worth roughly a 2x overstatement statewide. This tool calculates every formula that is defined, labels every line with its verification status and legal citation, and refuses to guess at the rules that aren't defined yet. Plan forward, based on the meeting

  1. Close the "no status" gap before trusting outputs. 7 of 20 rules in the workbook (Director, 11-Month Supervisor, Food Services Supervisor, Transportation Supervisor, Reading Cadre, Assistant Superintendent, Visiting Teacher, Driver Ed Teacher) have no entry in the Status column — meaning nobody has signed off that the "Implementable Business Rule" wording matches the statute. Get each one marked verified/pending/rejected by the business owner named in Sheet1 Q2 before the calculator ships numbers anyone relies on.
  2. Resolve BR-CO-009 (Reading Cadre) first — it's the one with a live dollar impact. The workbook itself documents a ~2x gap between the Calculator's $4.13M output and the $2.06M appropriation. This isn't a documentation nit, it's a formula bug with budget consequences. Get DDOE's confirmation on (a) the charter exclusion, (b) the §1707 per-unit equalization value replacing the flat $96,155, before anything downstream uses this line.
  3. Fill in the two empty categories. "Instructional Supports" and "Administrative Support Professionals" are listed as headers only — no formula, no citation, no business rule. Per Sheet1 Q3, several of these (Math Coaches, Early Literacy Coaches, Related Services, Opportunity Funding) are appropriation-funded line items, not Title 14 unit-generated positions, so they won't fit the same unit-based calculation pattern as the rest of this sheet. They need their own row structure (appropriation amount, distribution method, eligible LEA type) before they can go in a calculator.
  4. Load the missing salary schedules. Every compensation-type rule (principal, AP, superintendent, nurse, custodian, secretary) references §1305 / §1308 / §1311 dollar tables that aren't in this workbook. Without them the calculator can generate FTE counts but not costs. That's a second data-gathering task, separate from the unit-count rules.
  5. Wire the "tie every rule to Code" vision incrementally, not all at once. This calculator already links each rule's Column I citation straight to delcode.delaware.gov. The next layer — actual verification (e.g., pulling statute text and diffing it against the "Implementable Business Rule" wording automatically) — is worth scoping as a follow-on, but don't block the FTE/unit calculator on it. Ship what's citation-linked-but-manually-verified now; automate the verification step later once you know which rules keep drifting from statute (Reading Cadre suggests this will happen again).
  6. Decide who owns updating the workbook itself. Sheet1 Q2 asks this and it's still open. Until there's a named business owner for interpreting/approving each rule, every fix to this calculator is provisional — someone needs authority to say "yes, this implementation is correct" or the tool just re-encodes the same disputed logic in a new format.
